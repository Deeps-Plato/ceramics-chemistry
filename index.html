Why Is Wet Clay Plastic?

Kaolinite crystals are flat hexagonal platelets â€” like microscopic playing cards. When wet, water molecules intercalate between layers, acting as a lubricant that lets platelets slide over each other without breaking. This is plasticity.

As you dry clay, water leaves and the platelets bind together through van der Waals forces and hydrogen bonding â€” the piece stiffens. At this stage it is called leather-hard or bone-dry, and is still reversible: add water and it can become plastic again.

Key point: Before firing, clay is chemically just hydrated alumino-silicate. The irreversible transformation only happens above ~450 Â°C when the structural OH groups are permanently expelled.

How Reduction Works

In a gas or wood kiln, you can starve the chamber of oxygen by increasing fuel flow relative to air input. Carbon monoxide (CO) builds up:

CO + OÂ²â» (from oxide) â†’ COâ‚‚ + reduced metal

The CO strips oxygen from metal oxides, reducing them to lower oxidation states. Iron(III) Feâ‚‚Oâ‚ƒ â†’ Iron(II) FeO. Copper(II) CuO â†’ Copper(I) Cuâ‚‚O or even metallic Cu â€” giving the famous copper-red (ox-blood) glaze.

In an electric kiln, the elements heat without combustion, so the atmosphere is always oxidising â€” you get the "clean" oxidised colours. This is most home potters' reality.

Saggar reduction is a workaround: seal pots inside a ceramic box (saggar) with combustibles (sawdust, banana peel). Locally reducing atmosphere even in an electric kiln.

Safety: Reduction firing produces CO â€” always fire gas/wood kilns in well-ventilated outdoor or purpose-built spaces. Never attempt true reduction in an unvented indoor electric kiln.

Understanding the chemistry helps you avoid the most common failures and get consistent results from your home kiln.

Dry Slowly

Free water must leave before firing. Uneven drying = differential shrinkage = cracks. Cover pieces with plastic to slow the edges. Completely bone-dry before any heat.

Slow Through 573Â°C

The quartz inversion causes a sudden 2% volume change. Ramp â‰¤ 100Â°C/hr through 500â€“620Â°C, both going up AND cooling down. This is the #1 cause of dunting (cold cracks).

Don't Seal Carbon In

Organic matter must fully combust before the glaze seals the surface (~900Â°C). If you fire too fast, COâ‚‚ bubbles get trapped â†’ pinholes and crawling. Bisque first.

Test Glaze Thickness

Glaze chemistry assumes a specific application thickness (~1.5mm wet). Too thin = bare patches. Too thick = crawling or drips. Test tiles first.

Food Safety

Underfired glazes are porous and can leach colorants (Pb, Ba, Li). Always fire to the rated cone. All modern commercial glazes are lead-free, but verify before use on food surfaces. Check with a commercial glaze test service.

ğŸ§ª

Match Clay & Glaze COE

Coefficient of thermal expansion (COE) must match between clay and glaze. Glaze COE slightly lower than clay body = compression = good. Glaze COE higher = tension = crazing (fine cracks) or shivering (flaking).

Electric Kiln = Oxidation

Home electric kilns always fire in an oxidising atmosphere. Buy glazes rated for oxidation. Reduction glazes won't behave as labelled. Ventilate the room â€” clay, binders, and organics all off-gas during firing.

ğŸ“

Expect Shrinkage

Clay shrinks 8â€“15% during drying and firing (stoneware ~12%, porcelain up to 15%). This is water leaving + vitrification densifying the body. Design for it.

ğŸ›¡ï¸

Kiln Wash the Shelf

Kiln wash (alumina hydrate + kaolin + water) prevents glaze drips bonding to your shelf. The alumina has a very high melting point and won't fuse, acting as a sacrificial release layer.
